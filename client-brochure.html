1. Welcome

RAPS-ERP is a web-based enterprise resource planning platform built for manufacturing and laboratory operations. It connects your stores, purchase, quality, finance, and management teams in one place — eliminating spreadsheets, scattered emails, and paper trails.

What you can do from day one:
  • Raise and approve purchase requests across multiple units
  • Compare supplier quotations side-by-side and issue purchase orders
  • Track inward goods, QC inspections, and material issuance
  • Manage gate passes, inter-office notes, and inventory transfers
  • Audit every action with a full timestamped trail

2. How the System Works

RAPS-ERP follows a simple request → approve → act → record pattern. Every business action — buying a material, issuing stock from the warehouse, sending equipment off-site for repair — starts as a request, moves through one or more approvers, and ends with an automatic record in the audit log.

Seven user roles

AdminFull system access. Approves purchase requests and manages users.
ManagerRaises purchase requests, material issue vouchers, and gate passes for their unit.
Store ManagerHandles inward entry, stock movement, and warehouse logistics.
Purchase OfficerCollects quotations, places orders with suppliers, and tracks deliveries.
AccountingApproves payments, verifies invoices, and authorises gate pass closures.
QCConducts quality inspections on incoming goods and issues MIR reports.
LabR&D team — raises material requests and processes inter-office technical notes.
Multi-unitEach role is scoped to its unit; inter-unit transfers are tracked separately.

Built-in safeguards

  • Audit log — every create, approve, reject, and delete is permanently recorded with user name, time, and IP address.
  • Role-based access — users only see what their role permits.
  • Approval chains — high-value actions require multiple sign-offs.
  • Real-time notifications — approvers are alerted the moment a request is raised.
  • Document storage — attach PDFs (specs, quotations, test certificates) directly to any record.

3. Workflows in Plain English

Below is what each major workflow does and who is involved at each step.

1

Purchase Request & Purchase Order

What it does: Lets a unit raise a request for materials, get it approved, collect quotations, and place an order with the chosen supplier.

Flow: Manager raises PR → Admin approves quantities → Purchase Officer collects quotations → PO issued to supplier → Goods arrive → QC inspects → Inward entry closes the loop.

2

Material Issuance (MIV)

What it does: Allows production or lab teams to withdraw stock from the warehouse with proper authorisation.

Flow: Requester fills MIV → Store Manager approves and issues → Collector signs off → Stock auto-updates.

3

Gate Pass Management

What it does: Tracks assets leaving the premises — for repair, calibration, or external testing — and ensures they come back.

Flow: Unit Manager requests → Store Incharge arranges vehicle → Accounting approves payment terms → Final approval & dispatch → Return logged.

4

Quotation & Supplier Management

What it does: Maintains a supplier directory and lets Purchase Officers compare multiple bids before placing an order. Supports union quotations across units to negotiate better rates.

5

Payment Requests

What it does: Tracks advance, partial, and final payments against every PO. Accounting approves each payment request after invoice verification.

6

QC Inspection & MIR

What it does: Quality team inspects every incoming consignment, reviews supplier documents (COC, COA, test reports), accepts or rejects each batch, and issues the Material Inward Receipt.

7

Inventory Transfers

What it does: Moves stock between units with full approval and a complete trail of who moved what, when, and why.

8

Inter-Office Notes (ION)

What it does: Lets unit managers send technical notes to the lab along with samples and job details — and tracks the lab's response.

9

Product Catalog & Inventory

What it does: Central product database with SKU codes, current stock per unit, batch-wise tracking (FIFO), and live availability across the organisation.

6. How Future Updates Reach You

The system is updated centrally. There is no software for users to download or install — every change goes live for everyone at the same time.

Type of changeTime to appear for users
Small fix (text change, button tweak)Within 2 – 5 minutes of deployment
New featureWithin 5 – 10 minutes of deployment
Database schema changeWithin 5 – 15 minutes · brief restart possible
Major version upgradeScheduled in advance · planned maintenance window

How a typical update happens

  1. Developer makes the change in source code.
  2. Code is reviewed and pushed to the central code repository.
  3. Server pulls the new code, installs any new dependencies, and rebuilds the application.
  4. Application restarts (typically less than 5 seconds of downtime).
  5. Browsers refresh automatically — users see the new version the next time they reload the page.
What you have to do: nothing. If a user is in the middle of work when an update is deployed, they will simply see the new version after their next page refresh.

Maintenance windows

For larger changes that may involve a brief outage, we will give at least 24 hours' notice by email and schedule the work outside business hours (typically Sunday between 10 PM and 6 AM IST).
